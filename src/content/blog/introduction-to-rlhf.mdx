# Introduction

Reinforcement learning (RL) provides a principled framework for
optimizing sequential decision-making policies in stochastic
environments. In this context, an agent interacts with a Markov Decision
Process (MDP) and seeks to maximize an expected cumulative reward.

Policy gradient methods form a cornerstone of modern deep RL, allowing
the direct optimization of parameterized stochastic policies. These
methods provide:

- A formal gradient-based procedure for maximizing expected return,

- Flexibility to handle high-dimensional and continuous action spaces,

- Compatibility with function approximation using neural networks.

Despite their generality, vanilla policy gradient algorithms often
suffer from high variance and unstable updates. Actor-critic methods
mitigate these issues by introducing a learned value function to
estimate the advantage, while TRPO and PPO further stabilize policy
updates by controlling the effective step size in policy space.

Large-scale applications, such as training language models to follow
instructions, introduce additional challenges:

- Rewards are often not directly observed, requiring learned reward
  models,

- Value estimation is difficult or costly for high-dimensional discrete
  sequences,

- Stability and sample efficiency are crucial due to the scale of data
  and model size.

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) addresses these
challenges by combining:

1.  A pretrained or fine-tuned policy for initialization,

2.  A reward model learned from human preferences,

3.  Stable policy optimization methods such as PPO.

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) extends RLHF by removing the
critic entirely and leveraging group-relative baselines, providing a
more stable and computationally efficient method suitable for
large-scale language model alignment.

This document provides a comprehensive and rigorous treatment of:

- Policy gradient methods and the advantage formulation,

- Actor-critic algorithms and temporal-difference learning,

- Theoretical foundations including the performance difference lemma,

- Trust-region and proximal optimization methods (TRPO, PPO),

- Reinforcement learning from human feedback and group-relative
  approaches (RLHF, GRPO).

The exposition emphasizes formal derivations, clear definitions, and
detailed algorithmic insights, enabling implementation and practical
understanding from first principles.

# Foundations and Markov Decision Processes

This section establishes the mathematical framework underlying
reinforcement learning. We adopt a measure-theoretic perspective in
order to make expectations, probability distributions, and gradients
well-defined. All subsequent derivations build on these definitions.

## Measurable Spaces and Probability Kernels
Let 
$$
(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{F}_{\mathcal{S}})
$$
 and

$$
(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{F}_{\mathcal{A}})
$$
  denote measurable state and
action spaces, respectively. We assume that both spaces are standard
Borel spaces, which guarantees the existence of regular conditional
probabilities.

::: definition
**Definition 1** (Probability Kernel). *A probability kernel from
$\mathcal{X}$ to $\mathcal{Y}$ is a mapping

$$
K : \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{F}_{\mathcal{Y}} \to [0,1]
$$
 such
that:*

1.  *For each fixed $x \in \mathcal{X}$, $K(\cdot \mid x)$ is a
    probability measure on $\mathcal{Y}$.*

2.  *For each measurable set $B \subseteq \mathcal{Y}$, the function
    $x \mapsto K(B \mid x)$ is measurable.*
:::

Probability kernels allow us to define stochastic transitions and
policies in a mathematically precise way.

---

## Markov Decision Processes

::: definition
**Definition 2** (Discounted Markov Decision Process). *A discounted
Markov Decision Process (MDP) is a tuple

$$
\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, r, \rho, \gamma),
$$
where:*

- *$\mathcal{S}$ is the state space;*

- *$\mathcal{A}$ is the action space;*

- *$P(\cdot \mid s,a)$ is a probability kernel on $\mathcal{S}$, called
  the transition kernel;*

- *$r : \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the reward
  function;*

- *$\rho$ is a probability measure on $\mathcal{S}$, called the initial
  state distribution;*

- *$\gamma \in (0,1)$ is the discount factor.*
:::

The discount factor ensures that infinite-horizon returns are
well-defined. We assume throughout that the reward function is bounded,
i.e., 
$$
|r(s,a)| \leq R_{\max} < \infty,
\quad
\forall (s,a) \in \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A}.
$$
---

## Policies

::: definition
**Definition 3** (Stochastic Policy). *A stochastic policy is a
probability kernel 
$$
\pi(\cdot \mid s)
$$
 from $\mathcal{S}$ to
$\mathcal{A}$.*
:::

In this document, we focus on differentiable parametric policies.

::: definition
**Definition 4** (Parametric Policy). *A parametric policy is a family
of probability kernels

$$
\{\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s)\}_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d}
$$
 such that for
every $(s,a)$, the mapping $\theta \mapsto \pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ is
differentiable.*
:::

Typical examples include softmax policies in discrete action spaces and
Gaussian policies in continuous action spaces.

---

## Trajectories and Induced Probability Measures

A policy interacting with an MDP induces a probability distribution over
infinite trajectories.

::: definition
**Definition 5** (Trajectory). *A trajectory is an infinite sequence

$$
\tau = (s_0, a_0, s_1, a_1, s_2, a_2, \dots),
$$
 where
$s_t \in \mathcal{S}$ and $a_t \in \mathcal{A}$.*
:::

Given a policy $\pi_\theta$, the joint distribution over trajectories is
defined by 
$$
\mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)
=
\rho(s_0)
\prod_{t=0}^\infty
\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t).
$$
This expression should be interpreted as defining a probability measure
on the infinite product space

$$
(\mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A})^{\mathbb{N}}.
$$
---

## Return and Discounted Rewards

::: definition
**Definition 6** (Return). *The (discounted) return of a trajectory
$\tau$ is defined as 
$$
R(\tau)
=
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t, a_t).
$$
*
:::

Since rewards are bounded and $\gamma < 1$, the series converges
absolutely almost surely.

---

## Policy Objective

The goal of reinforcement learning is to find a policy that maximizes
the expected discounted return.

::: definition
**Definition 7** (Expected Return). *The performance of a policy
$\pi_\theta$ is defined as 
$$
J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \mathbb{P}_\theta}
\left[
R(\tau)
\right].
$$
*
:::

This expectation is taken jointly over:

- the initial state $s_0 \sim \rho$,

- the stochastic policy $\pi_\theta$,

- the environment dynamics $P$.

The dependence of $J(\theta)$ on $\theta$ is entirely through the
policy.

---

## Conditional Expectations and Markov Structure

A crucial property of MDPs is conditional independence.

::: proposition
**Proposition 1** (Markov Property). *Conditioned on $(s_t, a_t)$, the
next state $s_{t+1}$ is independent of all past states and actions.*
:::

This property allows us to define value functions and action-value
functions in terms of conditional expectations.

---

## State-Value and Action-Value Functions

::: definition
**Definition 8** (State-Value Function). *For a given policy $\pi$, the
state-value function is 
$$
V^\pi(s)
=
\mathbb{E}_\pi
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)
\;\middle|\;
s_0 = s
\right].
$$
*
:::

::: definition
**Definition 9** (Action-Value Function). *The action-value function is

$$
Q^\pi(s,a)
=
\mathbb{E}_\pi
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)
\;\middle|\;
s_0 = s, a_0 = a
\right].
$$
*
:::

Both expectations are taken with respect to the trajectory distribution
induced by $\pi$ and $P$.

---

## Bellman Equations

The value functions satisfy the Bellman equations.

::: lemma
**Lemma 1** (Bellman Expectation Equation). *For all
$s \in \mathcal{S}$, 
$$
V^\pi(s)
=
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}
\left[
r(s,a)
+
\gamma
\mathbb{E}_{s' \sim P(\cdot \mid s,a)}
[V^\pi(s')]
\right].
$$
*
:::

::: lemma
**Lemma 2** (Bellman Equation for $Q^\pi$). *For all $(s,a)$,

$$
Q^\pi(s,a)
=
r(s,a)
+
\gamma
\mathbb{E}_{s' \sim P(\cdot \mid s,a)}
\left[
\mathbb{E}_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')}
[Q^\pi(s',a')]
\right].
$$
*
:::

---

## Why This Formalism Matters

The explicit construction of probability measures and expectations is
essential for:

- justifying differentiation under the expectation sign,

- proving unbiasedness of gradient estimators,

- understanding variance reduction techniques,

- analyzing trust-region and preference-based objectives.

In the next section, we will build on this foundation to derive the
policy gradient theorem by differentiating the expected return with
respect to policy parameters.

# Policy Gradient Methods

In this section we derive the policy gradient theorem from first
principles. Our goal is to compute the gradient of the expected return

$$
J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \mathbb{P}_\theta}[R(\tau)]
$$
 with
respect to the policy parameters $\theta$.

Throughout this section, all expectations are taken with respect to the
trajectory distribution induced by the policy $\pi_\theta$ and the MDP
dynamics, unless otherwise stated.

---

## Differentiating Expectations over Trajectories

Recall that 
$$
J(\theta)=\int R(\tau)\, d\mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau).
$$
We assume that:

- $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ is differentiable in $\theta$,

- rewards are bounded,

- differentiation and integration can be interchanged.

Under these assumptions, we may write 
$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)=\nabla_\theta\int R(\tau)\, d\mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)=\int R(\tau)\, \nabla_\theta d\mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau).
$$
---

## Log-Derivative Trick

Using the identity 
$$
\nabla_\theta p(x) = p(x)\nabla_\theta \log p(x),
$$
we obtain 
$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\int R(\tau)\,
\nabla_\theta \log \mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)\,
d\mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \mathbb{P}_\theta}
\left[
R(\tau)\nabla_\theta \log \mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)
\right].
$$
This identity is the foundation of all policy gradient methods.

---

## Structure of the Trajectory Distribution

Recall that the trajectory probability factorizes as

$$
\mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)
=
\rho(s_0)
\prod_{t=0}^\infty
\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t,a_t).
$$
Only the policy terms depend on $\theta$. Therefore,

$$
\log \mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)
=
\log \rho(s_0)
+
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
+
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \log P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t,a_t).
$$
Taking the gradient yields 
$$
\nabla_\theta \log \mathbb{P}_\theta(\tau)=
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t).
$$
Substituting into the previous expression gives

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
R(\tau)
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\right].
$$
This expression is correct but suffers from extremely high variance.

---

## Causality and Reward-to-Go

We now exploit the temporal structure of the MDP to reduce variance.

Write the total return as $R(\tau)
=\sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k r(s_k,a_k).$

Substituting into the gradient expression: $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\sum_{k=0}^\infty
\gamma^k r(s_k,a_k)
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\right].$

We now separate the inner sum into two parts: $\sum_{k=0}^\infty=
\sum_{k=0}^{t-1}
+
\sum_{k=t}^\infty.$

---

### Causality Argument

::: lemma
**Lemma 3** (Causality). *For $k < t$, 
$$
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\gamma^k r(s_k,a_k)
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\right]
= 0.
$$
*
:::

::: proof
*Proof.* For $k < t$, the reward $r(s_k,a_k)$ depends only on
$(s_0,a_0,\dots,s_k,a_k)$, while
$\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$ depends only on
$(s_t,a_t)$.

By the Markov property, conditioned on $s_t$, the past is independent of
$a_t$. Moreover, 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{a_t \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t)}
\left[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\right]
=
\nabla_\theta
\int \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\, da_t
=
\nabla_\theta 1
=
0.
$$
 ◻
:::

---

### Reward-to-Go

By the causality lemma, only rewards from time $t$ onward contribute.
Define the reward-to-go: 
$$
G_t
=
\sum_{k=t}^\infty \gamma^{k-t} r(s_k,a_k).
$$
Then the policy gradient becomes 
$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
G_t
\right].
$$
This estimator has strictly lower variance than using the full return.

---

## Action-Value Function Interpretation

The reward-to-go is a random variable. We now express it in terms of a
conditional expectation.

::: definition
**Definition 10** (Action-Value Function). *
$$
Q^{\pi_\theta}(s,a)=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
G_0
\;\middle|\;
s_0 = s, a_0 = a
\right].
$$
*
:::

By time-shift invariance,

$$
\mathbb{E}[G_t \mid s_t, a_t] = Q^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t).
$$
Taking conditional expectation inside the sum yields the *policy
gradient theorem*.

---

## Policy Gradient Theorem

::: theorem
**Theorem 1** (Policy Gradient Theorem). *
$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
Q^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t)
\right].
$$
*
:::

This expression depends only on quantities local in time.

---

## Monte Carlo Estimation

In practice, the expectation is approximated using sampled trajectories 

$$
\{\tau^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^N \sim \mathbb{P}_\theta
$$
.

The Monte Carlo estimator is 
$$
\widehat{\nabla_\theta J}=
\frac{1}{N}
\sum_{i=1}^N
\sum_{t=0}^{T_i}
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t^{(i)} \mid s_t^{(i)})
G_t^{(i)}.
$$
By the law of large numbers, this estimator converges almost surely to
the true gradient as $N \to \infty$.

---

## Variance Considerations

Although unbiased, the estimator suffers from:

- long-horizon variance due to $G_t$,

- sensitivity to reward scaling,

- poor sample efficiency.

These issues motivate the introduction of baselines and advantage
functions, which we study in the next section.

# Baselines and Advantage Functions

The policy gradient theorem expresses the gradient of the expected
return as 
$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
Q^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t)
\right].
$$
While this estimator is unbiased, it typically exhibits high variance.
In this section, we introduce *baselines*, prove that they do not bias
the gradient, and show that the resulting formulation naturally leads to
the advantage function.

---

## Baselines and Unbiasedness

We begin by formalizing the notion of a baseline.

::: definition
**Definition 11** (Baseline). *A baseline is a measurable function

$$
b : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}
$$
 that depends only on the state.*
:::

Baselines are used to reduce variance in gradient estimates without
altering their expectation.

---

## Baseline Invariance Theorem

::: theorem
**Theorem 2** (Baseline Invariance). *For any measurable function
$b : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}$, 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\, b(s_t)
\right]
=
0.
$$
*
:::

::: proof
*Proof.* It suffices to prove that each term in the sum has zero
expectation. Fix $t$. Then 
$$
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\, b(s_t)
\right]
=
\mathbb{E}_{s_t}
\left[
b(s_t)
\mathbb{E}_{a_t \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t)}
\left[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\right]
\right].
$$
Since 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{a_t \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t)}
\left[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\right]
=
\nabla_\theta
\int \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\, da_t
=
\nabla_\theta 1
=
0,
$$
 the result follows. ◻
:::

---

## Baseline-Augmented Policy Gradient

Using the baseline invariance theorem, we may subtract any baseline from
the action-value function inside the policy gradient:


$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\bigl(
Q^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t) - b(s_t)
\bigr)
\right].
$$
This expression remains unbiased for any choice of $b$.

---

## State-Value Function as a Baseline

A particularly important choice of baseline is the state-value function.

::: definition
**Definition 12** (State-Value Function). *
$$
V^{\pi}(s)
=
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}
\left[
Q^{\pi}(s,a)
\right].
$$
*
:::

This definition is equivalent to the conditional expectation of the
return given the state: 
$$
V^\pi(s)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi}
\left[
G_0
\;\middle|\;
s_0 = s
\right].
$$
---

## Advantage Function

::: definition
**Definition 13** (Advantage Function). *The advantage function is
defined as 
$$
A^\pi(s,a)
=
Q^\pi(s,a) - V^\pi(s).
$$
*
:::

The advantage measures how much better (or worse) an action is compared
to the average action at a given state under policy $\pi$.

---

## Policy Gradient in Advantage Form

Using the state-value function as a baseline yields

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t)
\right].
$$
This is the standard form used in modern policy gradient methods.

---

## Why the Advantage is the Correct Signal

The advantage formulation has several important properties:

- $\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}[A^\pi(s,a)] = 0$ for all $s$,

- actions better than average produce positive updates,

- actions worse than average produce negative updates,

- variance is minimized among all state-dependent baselines.

In particular, the state-value function is the optimal baseline in the
mean-squared sense.

---

## Optimal Baseline (Variance Minimization)

Consider a fixed state $s$. The variance of the gradient estimator

$$
g(a) = \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s) (Q^\pi(s,a) - b(s))
$$
is minimized when 
$$
b(s) = V^\pi(s).
$$
This result justifies the use of the advantage function from a
variance-reduction perspective.

---

## Sample-Based Estimation

In practice, $Q^\pi$ and $V^\pi$ are unknown and must be estimated.

Given a sampled trajectory, we approximate:

$$
Q^\pi(s_t,a_t) \approx G_t,
\qquad
V^\pi(s_t) \approx \hat{V}(s_t).
$$
The resulting empirical advantage estimate is

$$
\hat{A}_t = G_t - \hat{V}(s_t).
$$
This estimator is unbiased if $\hat{V} = V^\pi$, and biased otherwise.

---

## Bias-Variance Tradeoff

Using an approximate value function introduces bias but dramatically
reduces variance. This tradeoff is at the heart of modern reinforcement
learning algorithms.

The next section formalizes this idea through actor-critic methods and
temporal-difference learning.

# Actor-Critic Methods and Temporal-Difference Learning

Policy gradient methods estimate 
$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t)
\right].
$$
However, computing the true advantage function requires knowledge of the
environment dynamics and the policy's long-term behavior. Actor-critic
methods address this by introducing a parametric approximation of the
value function, called the *critic*, and using it to construct a
low-variance estimator of the policy gradient.

---

## Separation of Roles: Actor and Critic

Actor-critic methods maintain two sets of parameters:

- **Actor parameters** $\theta$, defining the policy $\pi_\theta$

- **Critic parameters** $w$, defining a value function approximation
  $V_w \approx V^{\pi_\theta}$

The actor improves the policy by gradient ascent, while the critic
evaluates the current policy by solving an approximate Bellman equation.

---

## Value Function Approximation

::: definition
**Definition 14** (Parametric Value Function). *Let

$$
V_w : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}
$$
 be a differentiable function
approximator parameterized by $w \in \mathbb{R}^m$, intended to
approximate the true state-value function $V^{\pi_\theta}$.*
:::

The critic is trained to minimize the discrepancy between its
predictions and the Bellman target.

---

## Bellman Equation

The true value function satisfies the Bellman expectation equation:

$$
V^\pi(s)
=
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}
\left[
r(s,a)
+
\gamma
\mathbb{E}_{s' \sim P(\cdot \mid s,a)}
V^\pi(s')
\right].
$$
This fixed-point equation is the foundation of value-based learning.

---

## Temporal-Difference Learning

Rather than estimating $V^\pi$ via Monte Carlo returns, actor-critic
methods use bootstrapped updates.

::: definition
**Definition 15** (Temporal-Difference Error). *The temporal-difference
(TD) error at time $t$ is defined as 
$$
\delta_t
=
r_t + \gamma V_w(s_{t+1}) - V_w(s_t).
$$
*
:::

The TD error measures the instantaneous inconsistency of the critic with
respect to the Bellman equation.

---

## Critic Objective

The critic parameters $w$ are updated by minimizing the mean-squared TD
error: 
$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{critic}}(w)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}
\left[
\delta_t^2
\right].
$$
The gradient descent update is

$$
w \leftarrow w - \alpha_w \nabla_w \delta_t^2.
$$
---

## TD Error as an Advantage Estimator

The TD error is not equal to the advantage function, but its expectation
recovers it.

::: theorem
**Theorem 3** (TD Error and Advantage). *If $V_w = V^{\pi_\theta}$, then

$$
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\delta_t
\;\middle|\;
s_t = s, a_t = a
\right]
=
A^{\pi_\theta}(s,a).
$$
*
:::

::: proof
*Proof.* Conditioning on $s_t = s, a_t = a$,

$$
\mathbb{E}[\delta_t \mid s_t,a_t]
=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
r_t + \gamma V^{\pi_\theta}(s_{t+1}) - V^{\pi_\theta}(s_t)
\;\middle|\;
s_t,a_t
\right].
$$
By the definition of the action-value function, 
$$
Q^{\pi_\theta}(s,a)=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
r_t + \gamma V^{\pi_\theta}(s_{t+1})
\;\middle|\;
s_t = s, a_t = a
\right].
$$
Substituting yields 
$$
\mathbb{E}[\delta_t \mid s_t,a_t]=
Q^{\pi_\theta}(s,a) - V^{\pi_\theta}(s)
=
A^{\pi_\theta}(s,a).
$$
 ◻
:::

---

## Actor Update Using TD Error

Using the above result, the policy gradient may be written as

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t)
\right]
=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)
\mathbb{E}[\delta_t \mid s_t,a_t]
\right].
$$
Exchanging expectations yields the actor update:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\, \delta_t
\right].
$$
---

## Bias Introduced by an Imperfect Critic

When $V_w \neq V^{\pi_\theta}$, the TD error becomes a biased estimator
of the advantage: 
$$
\mathbb{E}[\delta_t \mid s_t,a_t]
\neq A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t,a_t).
$$
However, the variance reduction often outweighs this bias in practice.

---

## Two-Time-Scale Stochastic Approximation

Actor-critic methods rely on two learning rates:

$$
\alpha_w \gg \alpha_\theta.
$$
The critic converges rapidly to a neighborhood of $V^{\pi_\theta}$,
while the actor evolves slowly.

Under standard assumptions on step sizes and function approximation,
this scheme converges to a locally optimal policy.

---

## Online and Incremental Updates

Actor-critic methods support fully online updates: 
$$
\begin{align*}
\delta_t &= r_t + \gamma V_w(s_{t+1}) - V_w(s_t), \\
w &\leftarrow w + \alpha_w \delta_t \nabla_w V_w(s_t), \\
\theta &\leftarrow \theta + \alpha_\theta \delta_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t).
\end{align*}
$$
These updates interleave policy improvement and policy evaluation at
every step.

---

## Interpretation

Actor-critic methods can be viewed as:

- approximate policy iteration,

- stochastic gradient ascent on $J(\theta)$,

- bootstrapped advantage estimation.

They form the backbone of modern deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

---

## Limitations

Despite their success, actor-critic methods suffer from:

- instability from critic misestimation,

- sensitivity to learning rates,

- bias from bootstrapping,

- potential value function collapse.

These issues motivate trust-region methods and variance-controlled
estimators, which we introduce next.

# Discounted State Visitation and Performance Difference Lemma

Policy gradient methods optimize the expected return 
$$
J(\pi)=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)
\right].
$$
To reason about *finite* policy updates and their effect on performance,
we require a way to compare the value of two different policies. This
comparison is formalized by the performance difference lemma.

---

## Discounted State Visitation Distribution

We begin by defining the discounted state visitation distribution, which
plays a central role in policy comparison.

::: definition
**Definition 16** (Discounted State Visitation Distribution). *Let $\pi$
be a stationary policy. The discounted state visitation distribution
induced by $\pi$ is 
$$
d^\pi(s)
=
(1-\gamma)
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t
\mathbb{P}_\pi(s_t = s),
\qquad s \in \mathcal{S}.
$$
*
:::

---

## Properties of $d^\pi$

::: proposition
**Proposition 2**. *The distribution $d^\pi$ satisfies:*

1.  *$d^\pi(s) \ge 0$ for all $s$,*

2.  *$\sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} d^\pi(s) = 1$,*

3.  *For any bounded measurable function
    $f : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}$, 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi}[f(s)]
    =
    (1-\gamma)
    \mathbb{E}_\pi
    \left[
    \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t f(s_t)
    \right].
$$
*
:::

::: proof
*Proof.* The claims follow directly from the definition and Fubini's
theorem. ◻
:::

---

## Expected Return in Terms of $d^\pi$

The expected return can be rewritten as 
$$
J(\pi)=
\frac{1}{1-\gamma}
\mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi,\, a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}
\left[
r(s,a)
\right].
$$
This representation highlights that $J(\pi)$ depends on both the state
visitation frequencies and the action distribution.

---

## Action-Value Function Recap

Recall the definition of the action-value function: 
$$
Q^\pi(s,a)=
\mathbb{E}_\pi
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)
\;\middle|\;
s_0 = s, a_0 = a
\right].
$$
The value function satisfies 
$$
V^\pi(s)=
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}[Q^\pi(s,a)].
$$
---

## Advantage Function Recap

The advantage function is defined as 
$$
A^\pi(s,a)=
Q^\pi(s,a) - V^\pi(s).
$$
It measures the relative value of taking action $a$ at state $s$
compared to the average action under $\pi$.

---

## Performance Difference Lemma

We now state the main result.

::: lemma
**Lemma 4** (Performance Difference Lemma). *Let $\pi$ and $\pi'$ be two
stationary policies. Then 
$$
J(\pi') - J(\pi)=
\frac{1}{1-\gamma}
\mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^{\pi'}}
\left[
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'(\cdot \mid s)}
\left[
A^\pi(s,a)
\right]
\right].
$$
*
:::

---

## Proof of the Performance Difference Lemma

::: proof
*Proof.* Consider the quantity 
$$
\Delta_t
=
\gamma^t
\left(
r(s_t,a_t)
+
\gamma V^\pi(s_{t+1})
-
V^\pi(s_t)
\right).
$$
Taking expectations under $\pi'$, 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi'}[\Delta_t]=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi'}
\left[
\gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)
\right]
+
\mathbb{E}_{\pi'}
\left[
\gamma^{t+1} V^\pi(s_{t+1})
-
\gamma^t V^\pi(s_t)
\right].
$$
Summing over $t \ge 0$, the second term telescopes: 
$$
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\mathbb{E}_{\pi'}
\left[
\gamma^{t+1} V^\pi(s_{t+1})
-
\gamma^t V^\pi(s_t)
\right]=- \mathbb{E}_{s_0 \sim \rho}[V^\pi(s_0)].
$$
Therefore, 
$$
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \mathbb{E}_{\pi'}[\Delta_t]=
J(\pi') - J(\pi).
$$
Now condition on $s_t$: 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi'}[\Delta_t \mid s_t]=
\gamma^t
\left(
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'(\cdot \mid s_t)}
\left[
r(s_t,a)
+
\gamma \mathbb{E}_{s' \sim P}[V^\pi(s')]
\right]
-
V^\pi(s_t)
\right).
$$
By definition of the action-value function, 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'}
\left[
r(s_t,a)
+
\gamma \mathbb{E}_{s'} V^\pi(s')
\right]
=
V^\pi(s_t)
+
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'}[A^\pi(s_t,a)].
$$
Thus, 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi'}[\Delta_t]=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi'}
\left[
\gamma^t
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'(\cdot \mid s_t)}
[A^\pi(s_t,a)]
\right].
$$
Summing over $t$ and using the definition of $d^{\pi'}$,

$$
J(\pi') - J(\pi)
=
\frac{1}{1-\gamma}
\mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^{\pi'}}
\left[
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'}[A^\pi(s,a)]
\right].
$$
 ◻
:::

---

## Interpretation

The performance difference lemma shows that:

- Policy improvement depends on the expected advantage under the new
  policy,

- The expectation is taken over the new policy's state distribution,

- Exact evaluation requires knowledge of $d^{\pi'}$.

This result is exact but not directly usable for optimization.

---

## Implications for Policy Optimization

Since $d^{\pi'}$ depends on the new policy, direct optimization is
intractable. Trust-region methods approximate $d^{\pi'}$ with $d^\pi$ to
obtain a tractable surrogate objective.

This approximation is the foundation of TRPO and PPO.

# Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO)

## Motivation

Vanilla policy gradient methods perform unconstrained gradient ascent on

$$
J(\pi_\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\Big[\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)\Big].
$$
However, small changes in parameters $\theta$ can induce large changes
in the policy distribution $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$, potentially decreasing
performance.

The *Performance Difference Lemma* motivates updating the policy
conservatively:

$$
J(\pi') - J(\pi) = \frac{1}{1-\gamma} \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^{\pi'}} \big[ \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'}[A^\pi(s,a)] \big].
$$
Since the expectation is taken over $d^{\pi'}$, we cannot compute it
exactly without running $\pi'$. TRPO addresses this by:

1.  Approximating $d^{\pi'} \approx d^\pi$ for small policy changes,

2.  Adding a trust-region constraint to ensure $\pi'$ stays close to
    $\pi$.

---

## Surrogate Objective

Define the *surrogate objective* based on the current policy $\pi$:

$$
L_\pi(\pi') := \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi, a \sim \pi'}[A^\pi(s,a)].
$$
Using importance sampling, this can be rewritten as

$$
L_\pi(\pi') = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi, a \sim \pi} \Big[ \frac{\pi'(a \mid s)}{\pi(a \mid s)} A^\pi(s,a) \Big].
$$
This objective is tractable because it depends on $d^\pi$, which is
known from trajectories sampled under $\pi$.

---

## KL-Constrained Optimization Problem

To ensure that $\pi'$ does not deviate too far from $\pi$, TRPO imposes
a constraint using the KL divergence:

::: definition
**Definition 17** (Average KL Divergence).
*
$$
\bar{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi \| \pi') := \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi}\Big[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi(\cdot \mid s) \,\|\, \pi'(\cdot \mid s)) \Big].
$$
*
:::

The TRPO optimization problem is: 
$$
\begin{aligned}
\max_{\pi'} & \quad L_\pi(\pi') \\
\text{s.t.} & \quad \bar{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi \| \pi') \le \delta,
\end{aligned}
$$
 where $\delta > 0$ is a small constant specifying the
trust-region radius.

---

## Linearization and Quadratic Approximation

Let $\pi_\theta$ be parameterized by $\theta$. Linearizing the surrogate
objective around $\theta_0$ and using a second-order approximation for
the KL constraint yields:


$$
L_\pi(\pi_\theta) \approx L_\pi(\pi_{\theta_0}) + g^\top (\theta - \theta_0), \quad
\bar{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_{\theta_0} \| \pi_\theta) \approx \frac{1}{2} (\theta - \theta_0)^\top F (\theta - \theta_0),
$$
where 
$$
g = \nabla_\theta L_\pi(\pi_\theta)\big|_{\theta_0}, \quad
F = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi, a \sim \pi_\theta} \big[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s) \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s)^\top \big]
$$
is the Fisher information matrix.

The solution of the constrained optimization is the *natural gradient*
update: 
$$
\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k + \alpha F^{-1} g,
$$
 where $\alpha$ is
chosen to satisfy the KL constraint.

---

## Properties and Guarantees

- TRPO guarantees monotonic improvement:
  
$$
J(\pi') \ge J(\pi) - \mathcal{O}\Big(\frac{\gamma}{(1-\gamma)^2} \delta\Big),
$$
  provided $\delta$ is small.

- By controlling the KL divergence, TRPO prevents catastrophic drops in
  performance.

- The linearization and quadratic approximation make the update
  practical but require conjugate gradient methods and line search.

---

## Limitations

Despite its theoretical appeal, TRPO has practical drawbacks:

- Second-order optimization is computationally expensive,

- Requires large batch sizes for accurate Fisher estimates,

- Line search and conjugate gradient are complex to implement,

- Scaling to large neural networks is difficult.

These limitations motivate the simpler *Proximal Policy Optimization
(PPO)*, which we present next.

# Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)

## Motivation

While TRPO provides theoretical guarantees, it is computationally
expensive due to:

- Second-order optimization (Fisher matrix),

- Conjugate gradient and line search,

- Large batch requirements for stability.

PPO approximates TRPO with a first-order method that:

1.  Avoids explicit second-order computations,

2.  Maintains stability via a *clipped surrogate objective*,

3.  Can be efficiently implemented with standard stochastic gradient
    descent.

---

## Probability Ratio and Surrogate Objective

Let $\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}$ denote the current policy. Define the
probability ratio:


$$
r_t(\theta) := \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(a_t \mid s_t)}.
$$
The unclipped surrogate objective is:


$$
L^{\mathrm{PG}}(\theta) := \mathbb{E}\big[ r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t \big],
$$
where $\hat{A}_t$ is an estimator of the advantage function, e.g., Monte
Carlo return, TD error, or GAE.

---

## Clipped Objective

To prevent large policy updates that could destabilize training, PPO
introduces a clipping mechanism:

::: definition
**Definition 18** (PPO Clipped Objective).
*
$$
L^{\mathrm{PPO}}(\theta) := \mathbb{E} \Big[ \min \big( r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t, \; \mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_t \big) \Big],
$$
*

*where $\epsilon > 0$ is a hyperparameter controlling the maximum
allowed deviation of $r_t$ from $1$.*
:::

Intuition:

- If $\hat{A}_t > 0$, increasing $r_t$ beyond $1+\epsilon$ does not
  further increase the objective.

- If $\hat{A}_t < 0$, decreasing $r_t$ below $1-\epsilon$ does not
  further decrease the objective.

This enforces a *soft trust region* similar in spirit to TRPO, but in a
first-order, easily computable manner.

---

## Actor-Critic Variant with PPO

PPO is typically used in an actor-critic framework. Let $V_w(s)$ denote
the critic parameterized by $w$.

#### Advantage Estimation

The Generalized Advantage Estimator (GAE) is commonly used:


$$
\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V_w(s_{t+1}) - V_w(s_t), \quad
\hat{A}_t^{\mathrm{GAE}(\gamma,\lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}.
$$
Here, $\lambda \in [0,1]$ controls the bias-variance tradeoff:

- $\lambda = 0$: low-variance, one-step TD advantage,

- $\lambda = 1$: unbiased, high-variance Monte Carlo advantage.

#### Actor Objective

The actor maximizes the PPO clipped objective using
$\hat{A}_t = \hat{A}_t^{\mathrm{GAE}}$:


$$
L^{\mathrm{PPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E} \Big[ \min \big( r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t, \; \mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_t \big) \Big].
$$
#### Critic Objective

The critic minimizes the squared TD or Monte Carlo error:


$$
L_{\mathrm{value}}(w) = \mathbb{E} \big[ (V_w(s_t) - \hat{V}_t)^2 \big],
$$
where $\hat{V}_t$ is a bootstrapped return:


$$
\hat{V}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \gamma^l r_{t+l} \quad \text{or using TD bootstrap: } r_t + \gamma V_w(s_{t+1}).
$$
#### Entropy Regularization

An entropy bonus is often added to encourage exploration:


$$
L_{\mathrm{entropy}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E} \big[ \mathcal{H}(\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t)) \big].
$$
#### Combined Objective

The full PPO actor-critic objective is:


$$
L(\theta, w) = L^{\mathrm{PPO}}(\theta) - c_v L_{\mathrm{value}}(w) + c_e L_{\mathrm{entropy}}(\theta),
$$
where $c_v, c_e > 0$ are coefficients.

---

## Practical Implementation Notes

- Mini-batch stochastic gradient descent or Adam optimizer is used.

- Multiple epochs over the same batch can improve sample efficiency.

- GAE hyperparameters $(\gamma, \lambda)$ and clipping $\epsilon$ are
  tuned to balance stability and learning speed.

- PPO provides empirical robustness close to TRPO without requiring
  second-order optimization or line search.

---

## Summary

- PPO approximates TRPO using a first-order, clipped surrogate
  objective.

- Clipping ensures soft trust-region behavior, stabilizing updates.

- Advantage estimation (e.g., GAE) and entropy regularization improve
  learning efficiency.

- PPO is widely used in modern reinforcement learning due to its
  simplicity, scalability, and empirical performance.

# Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

## Motivation

Large language models (LLMs) pretrained with maximum likelihood
estimation (MLE) learn to model the distribution of text:

$$
\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \Big[ \sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}) \Big].
$$
However, MLE does not explicitly optimize for:

- Helpfulness,

- Safety,

- Alignment with human preferences.

RLHF introduces a learned reward function $r_\phi(x,y)$ that encodes
human judgment and optimizes the policy to maximize this reward using
reinforcement learning.

---

## Three Stages of RLHF

RLHF proceeds in three stages:

1.  **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):** Learn an instruction-following
    policy $\pi_{\mathrm{SFT}}$ using human demonstrations.

2.  **Reward Model (RM) Training:** Learn a reward model $r_\phi(x,y)$
    from human preference comparisons.

3.  **Policy Optimization:** Optimize $\pi_\theta$ to maximize expected
    reward using PPO.

---

## Stage I: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Given a dataset of prompt-response pairs $(x, y^*)$, train the policy
via maximum likelihood:


$$
\max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_{(x,y^*)} [ \log \pi_\theta(y^* \mid x) ].
$$
This yields an initial policy $\pi_{\mathrm{SFT}}$ that:

- Provides a reasonable starting point for RL,

- Serves as a reference policy for KL regularization.

---

## Stage II: Reward Model (RM) Training

### Preference Data

Humans provide relative feedback in the form of comparisons:


$$
(x, y^+, y^-), \quad \text{with } y^+ \succ y^-.
$$
This avoids absolute score calibration and aligns training with
comparative human judgments.

### Reward Model Architecture

The reward model $r_\phi(x,y)$ is typically:

- A transformer initialized from pretraining or SFT,

- Augmented with a scalar output head representing latent human
  preference,

- Fine-tuned on the preference dataset.

### Reward Model Loss

Using a Bradley-Terry or logistic formulation:


$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RM}}(\phi) = - \mathbb{E}_{(x, y^+, y^-)} \Big[ \log \sigma(r_\phi(x, y^+) - r_\phi(x, y^-)) \Big],
$$
where $\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$ is the logistic sigmoid. This
encourages $r_\phi(x, y^+) > r_\phi(x, y^-)$.

---

## Stage III: Policy Optimization with PPO

Treat $r_\phi(x,y)$ as a fixed reward function. Define the RL objective:


$$
J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)}[ r_\phi(x, y) ].
$$
Since rewards are non-differentiable with respect to discrete tokens,
policy gradient methods (e.g., PPO) are used.

#### KL Regularization

To prevent policy drift away from the SFT distribution:


$$
\tilde{r}_t = r_\phi(x_t, y_t) - \beta D_{\mathrm{KL}} \big( \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x_t) \| \pi_{\mathrm{SFT}}(\cdot \mid x_t) \big),
$$
with $\beta > 0$ controlling the strength of the penalty.

#### Advantage Estimation

Using a critic $V_w(s)$ and GAE:


$$
\delta_t = \tilde{r}_t + \gamma V_w(s_{t+1}) - V_w(s_t), \quad
\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^\infty (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}.
$$
#### Actor-Critic PPO Objective


$$
L(\theta, w) = \mathbb{E} \Big[ \min(r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t, \; \mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_t ) \Big] - c_v L_{\mathrm{value}}(w) + c_e \mathcal{H}(\pi_\theta).
$$
---

## Summary

- Pretraining learns general language modeling,

- SFT aligns the model to follow instructions,

- Reward model captures human preferences via comparisons,

- PPO optimizes the policy to maximize expected learned reward,

- KL regularization ensures stability and prevents distribution shift.

# Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)

## Motivation

PPO-based RLHF relies on a learned critic to estimate advantages.
However, this introduces:

- Bias when the critic is misestimated,

- Value function collapse in high-variance tasks,

- Extra computational cost.

GRPO addresses these issues by replacing the critic with a
*group-relative baseline*, leveraging multiple samples per prompt to
compute relative advantages.

---

## Group-Based Advantage

Given a prompt $x$, sample $K$ completions:


$$
\{y^{(1)}, \dots, y^{(K)}\} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x).
$$
The reward model assigns scores:


$$
R^{(k)} = r_\phi(x, y^{(k)}), \quad k=1,\dots,K.
$$
Define the group mean reward:


$$
\bar{R} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K R^{(k)}.
$$
::: definition
**Definition 19** (GRPO Advantage). *The group-relative advantage is

$$
\hat{A}^{(k)} = R^{(k)} - \bar{R}.
$$
*
:::

#### Key Property

By construction:


$$
\mathbb{E}_{k}[\hat{A}^{(k)} \mid x] = 0,
$$
so the baseline is unbiased, eliminating the need for a learned critic.

---

## GRPO Objective

Define the probability ratio:


$$
\rho^{(k)}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y^{(k)} \mid x)}{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(y^{(k)} \mid x)}.
$$
The GRPO objective mirrors PPO:


$$
L^{\mathrm{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E} \Big[ \min(\rho^{(k)}(\theta) \hat{A}^{(k)}, \; \mathrm{clip}(\rho^{(k)}(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}^{(k)}) \Big].
$$
This removes the critic entirely and relies on relative group
comparisons.

---

## Advantages of GRPO

- No critic required → avoids value estimation bias,

- Variance reduction via within-group comparison,

- Objective remains unbiased: 
$$
\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}^{(k)}] = 0,
$$
- Computationally simpler than PPO with a learned critic,

- Naturally aligns with human preference comparisons (relative reward).

---

## GRPO as PPO Without Critic

Conceptually, GRPO can be seen as:

::: center
*PPO where the value function $V(s)$ is replaced by an empirical group
mean $\bar{R}$.*
:::

This eliminates:

- TD bootstrapping bias,

- Critic collapse,

- Instability from misestimated value functions.

---

## Implementation Notes

- Sample multiple completions per prompt ($K \ge 4$ typically),

- Compute group-relative advantages and probability ratios,

- Apply PPO clipping as usual,

- No backpropagation through a critic is required.

---

## Summary

- GRPO simplifies RLHF by removing the critic,

- Uses group-relative baselines to reduce variance,

- Maintains unbiased policy gradient estimates,

- Provides stable and computationally efficient training for large-scale
  language models.

# GRPO Implementation for RLHF

The following pseudocode outlines the implementation of Group Relative
Policy Optimization (GRPO) applied in the context of Reinforcement
Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). We assume access to a pretrained
language model (e.g., GPT), a reward model trained on human preferences,
and tokenized input/output sequences.

## Setup

- **Actor (Policy) Model:** Pretrained transformer language model
  $\pi_\theta$ producing token probabilities.

- **Reward Model:** Pretrained or fine-tuned reward model $r_\phi$
  mapping sequences to scalar scores.

- **Data:** Prompts $x$ and model-generated completions $y$ (candidate
  responses).

- **Groups:** Partition of samples into groups $G_1, G_2, \dots$ for
  group-relative advantage computation.


## Pytorch Pseudocode
```python
# Pseudo-code: GRPO for RLHF

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.optim as optim

# ------------------------------------------------------------
# 1. Assumptions / Pretrained Components
# ------------------------------------------------------------

# We have a pretrained GPT model as the policy backbone.
# It outputs a distribution over the vocabulary at each token position.
# We'll denote it as `policy_model`.

class PolicyModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, pretrained_gpt):
        super().__init__()
        self.transformer = pretrained_gpt  # pretrained GPT
        # optionally add small policy head if needed
    def forward(self, input_ids, attention_mask=None):
        # returns logits over vocabulary
        return self.transformer(input_ids, attention_mask=attention_mask).logits

# Reward model: predicts scalar reward for a sequence (or token-wise).
# Usually a transformer head over hidden states, MLP pooling, then scalar output
class RewardModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_dim, vocab_size):
        super().__init__()
        self.transformer = pretrained_gpt  # can share weights or separate
        self.value_head = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, 1)
        )
    def forward(self, input_ids, attention_mask=None):
        hidden_states = self.transformer(input_ids, attention_mask=attention_mask).last_hidden_state
        # mean pooling
        pooled = hidden_states.mean(dim=1)
        reward = self.value_head(pooled).squeeze(-1)  # scalar reward per sequence
        return reward

# ------------------------------------------------------------
# 2. Data
# ------------------------------------------------------------

# Data comes from prompts and multiple responses from the policy:
# For GRPO, we group responses from several models or model samples to compute relative rewards
# We assume: batch of prompts `prompts`, each prompt has N sampled responses `responses`
# responses.shape = (batch_size, N, sequence_length)
# Each response is tokenized to `input_ids` for the policy and reward models

# Example:
# prompts = ["Write a polite email", "Summarize the article", ...]
# responses = model.sample_responses(prompts, num_samples=N)
# responses_tensor = tokenize(responses)  # shape [B, N, L]

# ------------------------------------------------------------
# 3. GRPO Algorithm
# ------------------------------------------------------------

# Key idea of GRPO:
# - Instead of using a critic (value function), we compute relative advantages
#   within a group of responses for the same prompt.
# - This removes the need for a separate value function.
# - Update policy using policy gradient weighted by these relative advantages.

# Hyperparameters
epsilon = 0.1  # PPO-like clipping
lr = 1e-5
num_epochs = 3
gamma = 0.99  # discount factor for sequence rewards if needed

# Instantiate models
policy_model = PolicyModel(pretrained_gpt)
reward_model = RewardModel(hidden_dim=768, vocab_size=50257)  # example for GPT-2

# Optimizer for policy
policy_optimizer = optim.Adam(policy_model.parameters(), lr=lr)

# ------------------------------------------------------------
# 4. Helper Functions
# ------------------------------------------------------------

def compute_group_advantages(rewards):
    """
    rewards: Tensor of shape (batch_size, N)
    Compute relative advantages by subtracting the mean reward in the group
    """
    group_mean = rewards.mean(dim=1, keepdim=True)
    advantages = rewards - group_mean  # shape (B, N)
    return advantages

def get_log_probs(policy_model, input_ids):
    """
    Compute log-probabilities of token sequences under current policy
    input_ids: shape (B, L)
    Returns: log_probs per sequence, shape (B,)
    """
    logits = policy_model(input_ids)  # [B, L, vocab_size]
    log_probs = torch.log_softmax(logits, dim=-1)
    # gather log probs for each token
    token_log_probs = log_probs.gather(-1, input_ids.unsqueeze(-1)).squeeze(-1)  # [B, L]
    return token_log_probs.sum(dim=1)  # sum over sequence -> [B]

# ------------------------------------------------------------
# 5. Training Loop
# ------------------------------------------------------------

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for batch_prompts, batch_responses in data_loader:  # batch_responses: (B, N, L)
        
        B, N, L = batch_responses.shape
        
        # Flatten batch for policy evaluation
        input_ids_flat = batch_responses.view(B*N, L)
        
        # Compute rewards for each response
        with torch.no_grad():
            rewards_flat = reward_model(input_ids_flat)  # [B*N]
        rewards = rewards_flat.view(B, N)  # reshape [B, N]
        
        # Compute group-relative advantages
        advantages = compute_group_advantages(rewards)  # [B, N]
        advantages_flat = advantages.view(B*N)
        
        # Compute log probabilities under current policy
        log_probs_flat = get_log_probs(policy_model, input_ids_flat)  # [B*N]
        
        # GRPO: policy gradient weighted by relative advantage
        # We can optionally clip ratio as in PPO
        with torch.no_grad():
            old_log_probs_flat = log_probs_flat.clone()  # for ratio
        
        # Probability ratio for PPO-style clipping
        ratio = torch.exp(log_probs_flat - old_log_probs_flat)
        clipped_ratio = torch.clamp(ratio, 1-epsilon, 1+epsilon)
        # surrogate objective
        surrogate = torch.min(ratio * advantages_flat, clipped_ratio * advantages_flat)
        loss = -surrogate.mean()  # gradient ascent -> minimize negative
        
        # Backprop and update policy
        policy_optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss.backward()
        policy_optimizer.step()
```

## Notes on Implementation

- The **policy model** is typically a transformer (e.g., GPT) that
  outputs a probability distribution over the next token.

- The **reward model** can be an MLP or transformer that outputs a
  scalar for a full sequence.

- The **group-relative baseline** stabilizes training by centering
  rewards within each group, reducing variance without requiring a
  critic.

- The **clip** operation enforces a soft trust region similar to PPO.

- Sequences can be truncated or padded for efficient batching.
